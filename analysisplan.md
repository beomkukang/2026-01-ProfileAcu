How to "Coordinate" the Body Profile of rats?

Fig 2 — Bayes Factor Heatmap (Disease + Mechanism)
Seaborn core: sns.heatmap × 2 panels (stacked), shared color scale, RdBu_r centered at 0 on log₂(BF)
Key design decision: Annotate only cells exceeding BF > 3 or BF < 0.25 thresholds; mask zero-record cells in gray; rows ordered by hierarchical clustering, not by anatomical pair; cluster membership shown by colored sidebar
What it depicts: The upper panel shows disease-domain enrichment for each site relative to the pooled baseline. The lower panel shows the same for mechanism domains. Red cells mean a site is studied for that domain more than expected; blue cells mean less than expected; white means the site matches the population average. The row ordering groups functionally similar sites together — the reader sees cluster structure emerging from the enrichment patterns.
How to interpret: This is the core characterization figure. The reader identifies each site's functional identity: GV4 is a neurological specialist (one intense red cell, everything else blue or gray), PC6 is a cardiovascular specialist, ST25 and BL25 are gastrointestinal, ST36 is a generalist (entire row near white — no enrichment or depletion anywhere). The disease panel will show sharp differentiation; the mechanism panel will look more muted (more white, fewer extreme cells) — this visual contrast is the first hint of mechanism-space compression. The reader should also notice that anatomically paired sites (e.g., BL25 and GV4) are not adjacent in the row ordering — they've been separated by the clustering algorithm because their enrichment patterns are dissimilar.

Fig 3 — Three Representational Dissimilarity Matrices
Seaborn core: sns.heatmap × 3 panels (side by side); viridis colormap for disease and mechanism RDMs (shared scale, 0–1 cosine distance); magma for somatotopic RDM (different metric, different scale)
Key design decision: Rows and columns ordered by disease-space cluster (same ordering across all three panels for direct visual comparison); annotate cell values on disease and mechanism panels only; mask diagonal; highlight anatomical pairs with border rectangles
What it depicts: Three 8×8 matrices showing pairwise distances between all sites in each representational space. The disease RDM shows who treats similar conditions. The mechanism RDM shows who engages similar biological pathways. The somatotopic RDM shows who is physically close on the body. These are the primary analytical objects — everything downstream derives from comparing these matrices.
How to interpret: The reader should compare the three matrices by scanning for structural correspondence. Disease and mechanism RDMs should show similar block structure — if two sites are close in disease space (dark cell), they should also be close in mechanism space (dark cell in the same position). The somatotopic RDM should show a different pattern — anatomically paired sites are close (dark cells along predictable positions) but this structure shouldn't map onto the functional RDMs. The most dramatic example: BL25–GV4 will be dark (close) in the somatotopic RDM but bright (far) in both functional RDMs. The mechanism RDM will appear overall darker and more uniform than the disease RDM — this is the compression effect: sites are more similar in how they work than in what they treat.

Fig 4 — RSA Scatterplots (RDM-to-RDM Comparisons)
Seaborn core: sns.scatterplot × 3 panels; within-pair points in red, cross-pair in blue; key pairs labeled; sns.regplot trend line on significant panel only
Key design decision: Add permutation null distribution band (shaded 95% CI from 10,000 permutations) on the non-significant panels so the null result reads as "confirmed absence" rather than "failed analysis"; report Spearman r and permutation p prominently in each panel
What it depicts: Each panel plots the 28 pairwise distances from one RDM against another. Left panel: disease distance vs. mechanism distance (the convergence test). Middle panel: somatotopic distance vs. disease distance (the anatomy-disease independence test). Right panel: somatotopic distance vs. mechanism distance (the anatomy-mechanism test). Each dot is one pair of sites.
How to interpret: The left panel is the core finding — a positive correlation means sites that treat similar diseases engage similar mechanisms. The trend line and significant p-value confirm this. The reader should look for labeled outliers: BL25–ST25 should appear in the bottom-left (close in both disease and mechanism — gastrointestinal convergence despite anatomical distance), while BL25–GV4 should appear with low disease similarity but variable mechanism position. The middle panel should show no trend — a flat cloud within the permutation null band, meaning knowing where a site is on the body tells you nothing about what diseases it treats. The right panel is the ambiguous case (simple Mantel may be significant but partial Mantel after controlling for disease is not) — the reader should see a weak trend that the text explains is mediated by disease. Red dots (anatomical pairs) should not cluster in any consistent region of any panel — if anatomy drove function, they'd all be in the bottom-left corner, but they're scattered.

Fig 5 — Disease–Mechanism Coupling
Seaborn core: Panel A: sns.barplot for top-3 concentration, ordered by concentration value, colored by cluster. Panel B: bubble plots of the disease × mechanism contingency table for 3 exemplar sites (GV4, PC6, ST36) using sns.scatterplot with size mapped to record count
Key design decision: Replace NMI vs. entropy scatter (requires information theory literacy) with more intuitive display; bubble contingency tables show the actual joint distribution rather than a summary statistic
What it depicts: Panel A shows what percentage of each site's records fall into just three disease–mechanism cells. High bars mean a site's research is concentrated in a few specific disease–mechanism combinations; low bars mean it's spread across many. Panel B shows what those combinations are — each bubble sits at the intersection of a disease category (y-axis) and mechanism category (x-axis), with bubble size proportional to record count.
How to interpret: Panel A establishes the specialist–generalist continuum at a glance. GV4's bar at ~60% means most of its research is concentrated in three cells. ST36's bar at ~20% means its research is broadly distributed. Panel B explains what this means concretely: GV4's contingency table has one dominant bubble (Neurological × Neuroprotection) and near-emptiness elsewhere — this site does one thing through one pathway. PC6 has a dominant disease column (Cardiovascular) but multiple mechanism rows populated — this site treats one disease through multiple pathways. ST36 has many small bubbles scattered everywhere — no dominant disease–mechanism pairing. The reader should come away understanding that sites differ not just in what they treat (disease profiles) or how they work (mechanism profiles) but in how tightly those two dimensions are linked within each site.

Fig 6 — Low-Dimensional Embeddings
Seaborn core: sns.scatterplot × 2 panels (disease-space embedding left, mechanism-space embedding right); color by cluster, markers labeled with site names; anatomical pairs connected by dashed matplotlib lines
Key design decision: Consider CA biplot instead of pure MDS (CA shows disease/mechanism category positions alongside site positions, making the space interpretable without cross-referencing Fig 2); add bootstrap confidence ellipses per cluster; report variance explained in axis labels
What it depicts: Each point is one stimulation site, positioned so that sites with similar profiles are close together and sites with different profiles are far apart. Left panel shows disease-space geometry; right panel shows mechanism-space geometry. Dashed lines connect anatomically paired sites. If the embedding uses CA biplot format, disease/mechanism category names appear as labeled triangles showing which categories pull each site toward its position.
How to interpret: The reader should look for three things. First, cluster separation: in disease space, the GI cluster (ST25, BL25), cardiovascular outlier (PC6), and broad cluster should occupy distinct regions. In mechanism space, the same sites should be more compressed — closer together, with less separation between clusters. Second, anatomical pair lines: these dashed lines should cross cluster boundaries in disease space (paired sites end up in different functional neighborhoods), visually demonstrating that anatomy does not predict disease-space position. Third, if using CA biplot format, the reader can trace why each site is where it is — PC6 sits near the "Cardiovascular" label, BL25 near "Gastrointestinal," GV4 near "Neurological." This makes the embedding self-explanatory rather than requiring the reader to cross-reference the BF heatmap. The confidence ellipses tell the reader how stable each point's position is — large ellipses for small-n sites (BL25, GV4) honestly communicate that their positions carry more uncertainty.
